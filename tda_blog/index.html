Part I
The Mathematics of Shape
Section 1.1

Where It All Began — Euler and the Seven Bridges of Königsberg

The year is 1736. In the Prussian city of Königsberg (modern-day Kaliningrad), seven bridges connect two islands in the Pregel River to the surrounding mainland. The citizens pose a deceptively simple puzzle:
Is it possible to walk through the city, crossing each bridge exactly once, and return to the starting point?

The great mathematician Leonhard Euler realized that the specific distances, positions, and shapes of the land masses were irrelevant. What mattered was only the structure of connections — how many bridges connected each region. He abstracted the map into a graph: four nodes (land masses) connected by seven edges (bridges).

Euler's Theorem (1736). A connected graph has an Eulerian circuit (a closed walk visiting every edge exactly once) if and only if every vertex has even degree. In Königsberg, all four vertices had odd degree — so the answer was no.

This proof is considered the birth of topology. Euler showed that some problems depend not on geometry but on something more fundamental — the qualitative structure of connections. This is exactly what topology studies: properties of shape that survive when you stretch, bend, or deform an object, but never tear or glue.

A coffee cup and a donut are topologically identical (both have exactly one hole). A sphere and a cube are also identical (zero holes). What matters is not the shape's appearance but its connectivity — how many holes, components, and voids it contains.

Nearly 300 years later, this same idea — that abstract structural features convey more vital intelligence than raw measurements — serves as the foundation of Topological Data Analysis. But how do we apply an 18th-century puzzle about static bridges to the chaotic, high-velocity fluctuations of modern financial markets?

Section 1.2

Topological Data Analysis — Seeing Beyond Statistics

By shifting our focus from the physical geography of bridges to the abstract geometry of data. Traditional data analysis asks: "What is the average? What is the variance?"
Topological Data Analysis asks a fundamentally different question: "What is the shape of the data?"

TDA brings Euler's topological thinking into the 21st century. Given a point cloud $X = \{x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n\} \subset \mathbb{R}^d$, it asks: does this cloud have clusters? Loops? Voids? These features — formally called topological invariants — reveal structural patterns that are completely invisible to correlation matrices, PCA, or neural networks.

This is our first detective tool. But there's a problem: TDA requires a multi-dimensional point cloud, and financial data is just a flat, one-dimensional ticker tape. To proceed, we need a way to manufacture geometry from a single sequence of prices.

Section 1.3

Takens' Time-Delay Embedding

The answer comes from an extraordinary result in dynamical systems theory. Takens' Embedding Theorem (1981) proves that you can faithfully reconstruct the topology of a dynamical system's attractor from a single observed variable — using nothing more than time-delay coordinates. In plain terms: a flat price chart contains enough hidden information to rebuild the market's full geometric shape.

Takens' Theorem. Given a smooth dynamical system and a generic observation function, the map $\Phi: \mathcal{M} \to \mathbb{R}^{2m+1}$ defined by time-delay embedding is a diffeomorphism on the attractor, where $m$ is the attractor's box-counting dimension.

In practice, given a price series $P = \{p_1, p_2, \dots, p_N\}$, we construct vectors:

$$ \mathbf{v}_t = \begin{bmatrix} p_t \\ p_{t+\tau} \\ p_{t+2\tau} \\ \vdots \\ p_{t+(d-1)\tau} \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^d $$

Here, $d$ is the embedding dimension (how many coordinates per vector) and $\tau$ is the time delay (the spacing between observations). Each vector $\mathbf{v}_t$ becomes a single point in $d$-dimensional space. The collection of all these points forms our point cloud.

A flat, one-dimensional price line has been transformed into a multi-dimensional geometric object. We finally have a shape that topology can analyze. But a cloud of scattered points is formless on its own — we need a mathematical rule that decides which points to connect. What are the building blocks of that connection?

Section 1.4

Simplicial Complexes — Building Blocks of Shape

The building blocks are called simplices — the geometric primitives from which all topological shapes are assembled. To measure the "shape" of a point cloud, we need to connect the dots, and the mathematical scaffolding for this is the simplicial complex — a combinatorial structure built from elementary pieces called $k$-simplices:

  • 0-simplex: A vertex (a single point)
  • 1-simplex: An edge (a line segment connecting two points)
  • 2-simplex: A triangle (three connected points with a filled face)
  • 3-simplex: A tetrahedron (four points forming a solid)

In general, a $k$-simplex is the convex hull of $k+1$ affinely independent points. A simplicial complex $\mathcal{K}$ is a collection of simplices that is closed under taking faces — if a triangle is in $\mathcal{K}$, then all three of its edges and all three vertices must also be in $\mathcal{K}$.

$$ \begin{aligned} &\sigma = [v_0, v_1, \dots, v_k] \quad \text{is a } k\text{-simplex} \\ &\mathcal{K} \text{ is a simplicial complex if } \forall \sigma \in \mathcal{K}, \; \text{every face of } \sigma \in \mathcal{K} \end{aligned} $$

We now have the vocabulary of shape. But a critical question remains: given a cloud of market-state points, which simplices do we actually build? How close must two points be before we connect them with an edge?

Section 1.5

The Vietoris-Rips Complex

The answer is a single parameter: a distance threshold $\epsilon$. The Vietoris-Rips complex provides an elegant, parameter-driven rule — connect any set of points whose pairwise distances all fall within $2\epsilon$.

Definition. The Vietoris-Rips complex $VR_\epsilon(X)$ includes a simplex $[x_{i_0}, \dots, x_{i_k}]$ if and only if $d(x_{i_p}, x_{i_q}) \leq 2\epsilon$ for all pairs $0 \leq p, q \leq k$.

Picture a sphere of radius $\epsilon$ inflating outward from every data point simultaneously. At $\epsilon = 0$, all points are isolated — no connections. As $\epsilon$ grows, spheres overlap, edges appear, triangles form, and higher-dimensional simplices crystallize. At $\epsilon = \infty$, everything merges into a single, featureless blob.

The real structure lives in the intermediate scales — where the complex reveals the true geometric fingerprint of the data. But this raises a dangerous question: which value of $\epsilon$ is the "right" one? Picking a single threshold would be arbitrary guesswork. We need a way to study the shape at every scale simultaneously.

Fig 4. The Vietoris-Rips complex evolving as the distance threshold ε increases.

Section 1.6

Persistent Homology — The Memory of Shape

This is exactly what Persistent Homology does. Instead of committing to a single $\epsilon$, it studies the entire movie — the complete sequence of complexes as $\epsilon$ sweeps from $0$ to $\infty$. This growing sequence is called a filtration:

$$ \emptyset = \mathcal{K}_0 \subseteq \mathcal{K}_1 \subseteq \mathcal{K}_2 \subseteq \dots \subseteq \mathcal{K}_m $$

As we traverse this filtration, topological features are born (they appear) and die (they get filled in or merge). Each feature is tracked as a pair:

$$ (b_i, d_i) \quad \text{where } b_i = \text{birth scale}, \; d_i = \text{death scale} $$ $$ \text{Persistence} = d_i - b_i $$

A feature with high persistence (large $d_i - b_i$) is a genuine structural signal — it survived across many scales. A feature that flickers in and out is noise. This is how persistent homology separates signal from noise — without any parameter tuning.

Betti Numbers: Counting the Features

We quantify what persistent homology finds using Betti numbers ($\beta_k$) — the rank of the $k$-th homology group $H_k$. Each one counts a different type of topological feature:

  • $\beta_0$ = number of connected components (clusters). How fragmented is the market?
  • $\beta_1$ = number of 1-dimensional loops (cycles). Is the market trapped in a feedback cycle?
  • $\beta_2$ = number of 2-dimensional voids (cavities). Are there hollow zones where liquidity has evaporated?

The collection of all $(b_i, d_i)$ pairs forms a persistence diagram — a complete topological fingerprint of the data's shape at every scale. The theory is now complete. We have the tools: Takens embedding creates the point cloud, simplicial complexes give it structure, and persistent homology reads its shape. The question that remains is practical: can this mathematics actually detect a market crash?

Section 2.1

The TDA Pipeline — From Price to Prediction

The TDA Market Risk Analyzer chains together the three mathematical tools from Part I into a single, automated pipeline that runs on every uploaded financial dataset:

Stage 1: Takens Embedding

A rolling window (default: 50 data points) slides across the price series. Within each window, the closing prices are normalized and embedded into $\mathbb{R}^d$ using Takens' delay coordinates. The result is a localized point cloud representing the market's current geometric state.

Stage 2: Persistent Homology via Vietoris-Rips

The application builds a Vietoris-Rips filtration on each embedded window and computes persistent homology using one of three backends:

  • Giotto-TDA — production-grade, optimized C++ bindings
  • Ripser — fast, memory-efficient Ripser algorithm
  • Scipy Fallback — heuristic approximation, zero dependencies

Stage 3: Risk Index = L₁ Persistence Norm

For each window, the system computes the total persistence of all $H_1$ features (1-dimensional loops):

$$ \text{Risk Index}(t) = \sum_{i} (d_i - b_i) \quad \text{for all } H_1 \text{ features in window } t $$

A dynamic threshold is computed as the statistical boundary between normal volatility and structural danger:

$$ \text{Threshold} = \mu_{\text{risk}} + 2\sigma_{\text{risk}} $$

When the Risk Index crosses this threshold, the system flags an Instability Zone — the market's topological structure is collapsing. The pipeline produces a concrete, binary signal. But this raises a deeper question: why do topological loops spike right before a crash? What is the financial mechanism behind this geometric phenomenon?

Section 2.2

Why Loops Predict Crashes

Because a topological loop is the geometric signature of a feedback loop — a system caught in a self-reinforcing spiral. During stable market conditions, the Takens-embedded point cloud forms smooth, consistent curves. The $H_1$ features (loops) have moderate, stable persistence.

But as a crash approaches, something remarkable happens:

  • Asset correlations spike — everything starts moving together
  • The underlying dynamical system loses its degrees of freedom
  • The point cloud's geometry undergoes a phase transition
  • Loops rapidly form and collapse — their total persistence spikes

This spike in the Risk Index precedes the actual price drop by several days. The topology sees the structural fracture before the market reacts. This is the fundamental insight: geometric collapse precedes price collapse.

The mathematics works. The final step is to package it into a tool that any analyst can use.
